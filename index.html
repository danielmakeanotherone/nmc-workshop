Group 01 — Defend Your Person
B.R. ShettyFounder & Former Co-Chairman, NMC Health
Least Responsible?
Who Is He
B.R. Shetty founded NMC Health in 1974 as a small clinic in Abu Dhabi. He built it into the largest private healthcare network in the UAE, eventually listing on the London Stock Exchange and reaching the FTSE 100 with an $11 billion valuation. At his peak, Forbes estimated his personal net worth at $4.2 billion. When the fraud was exposed in 2020, Shetty stepped down and denied all knowledge of the hidden debt — claiming he was deceived by the very executives he trusted to run the company.
Key Facts for Your Defense
  • Shetty claims his signature was electronically forged on loan documents and bank transfers — a claim he presented as evidence in the ongoing $5.6 billion criminal trial.
  • The presiding judge rejected the forged signature claim, but Shetty maintains that a small group of former executives orchestrated the fraud behind his back.
  • Shell companies were allegedly set up in his name without his knowledge or authorization, according to an internal investigation he himself commissioned.
  • He was not the only executive managing day-to-day financial operations — CEO Prasanth Manghat and others had direct operational control over the books.
  • He voluntarily stepped down, cooperated with authorities, and filed his own lawsuits against former executives he claims defrauded him.
  • EY, the professional external auditor, signed off on the financial statements for years — if the founder trusted the same auditors the banks trusted, can he be blamed for believing his own company's books?
The Argument Against You

The prosecution will argue: As founder and chairman, Shetty had ultimate responsibility for the company. The fraud benefited him directly — billions were redirected into high-end real estate, private jets, and luxury vehicles. It is nearly impossible for a hands-on founder to be unaware of a $6.6 billion discrepancy in his own company over many years.

You need to counter this. Think about: what does it mean to delegate authority? Is a CEO always personally guilty of what their employees do?

Guiding Questions
01
If Shetty truly didn't know, who should have told him — and why didn't they?
Think about the role of auditors, the board, and internal controls.
02
Is it possible to run a $11 billion company and genuinely not know $6.6 billion is missing?
Consider how large corporations are structured and how information flows up.
03
What is the single strongest reason Shetty bears less responsibility than the other three parties?
Build toward your closing argument — what's the core of your case?

Group 02 — Defend Your Person
Ernst & YoungExternal Auditor — Big Four Accounting Firm
Least Responsible?
Who Are They
Ernst & Young (EY) is one of the Big Four global accounting firms, responsible for independently auditing NMC Health's financial statements and certifying they were accurate. An external auditor's job is to verify a company's books are truthful — acting as a check on management. EY signed off on NMC's accounts for years, issuing a "Going Concern" approval that told the world NMC was financially stable. They later settled a £105.5 million negligence lawsuit without admitting any wrongdoing.
Key Facts for Your Defense
  • NMC staff refused to allow EY auditors to download financial data directly — they were only permitted to view records on screens controlled by NMC personnel, making independent verification nearly impossible.
  • When EY auditors attempted to contact suppliers directly to verify invoice details, NMC personnel intercepted and provided forged documentation that passed inspection.
  • EY auditors who pushed back with difficult questions were threatened with termination of the firm's contract — creating intense pressure to back down.
  • Auditors can only audit what they are given access to. If a client actively deceives, blocks, and forges documents, the auditor's ability to detect fraud is fundamentally compromised.
  • NMC deliberately leveraged EY's prestigious reputation with banks — banks trusted EY's clean sign-off, not NMC's word. EY was weaponized without their knowledge.
  • EY settled the lawsuit without admitting liability — meaning legally, no court has ruled they were definitively negligent.
The Argument Against You

The prosecution will argue: EY was paid to catch exactly this kind of fraud. Red flags existed in public financial statements — Muddy Waters Research found them without any insider access at all. If a short-seller could spot the inconsistencies, a professional audit firm had no excuse. EY also had financial motivation to keep the contract, which compromised their independence.

You need to counter this. Think about: what is an auditor actually responsible for versus what is management's responsibility?

Guiding Questions
01
If EY was blocked from accessing real data, whose fault is it that the fraud wasn't caught?
Consider what auditors are legally empowered to demand from a client.
02
Should EY have walked away from the contract when they were threatened? What would have happened if they did?
Think about what refusing to sign off would signal to the market.
03
What is the single strongest reason EY bears less responsibility than the other three parties?
Build toward your closing argument — what's the core of your case?

Group 03 — Defend Your Person
The Banks80+ Global Lenders — Including ADCB, HSBC, Barclays
Least Responsible?
Who Are They
Over 80 banks globally lent money to NMC Health over many years, including Abu Dhabi Commercial Bank (owed ~$1 billion), Dubai Islamic Bank ($541M), Standard Chartered ($250M), and Barclays ($146M). Banks lend based on a company's financial health — they review audited statements, credit ratings, and market standing. NMC was a FTSE 100 company with a clean EY audit, making it appear to be one of the safest borrowers in the region. The banks ended up as some of the biggest financial victims of the fraud.
Key Facts for Your Defense
  • Every bank relied on EY's audited financial statements — the same documents that a Big Four firm had professionally certified as accurate. Banks are not accounting firms; they depend on auditors to do that job.
  • NMC was listed on the London Stock Exchange and part of the FTSE 100 — one of the most scrutinized and prestigious indices in the world. Being FTSE 100 signals credibility, regulatory oversight, and transparency.
  • The banks were themselves victims — they lost billions. Abu Dhabi Commercial Bank alone was exposed to nearly $1 billion in debt they may never fully recover.
  • The fraud used "reverse factoring" — a legitimate financial tool — to disguise debt as normal supplier payment arrangements. This made the borrowing look like routine trade financing, not alarm-worthy debt.
  • Multiple regulators including the FRC were also watching NMC and found nothing — if trained regulators missed it, banks had even less reason to suspect fraud.
  • Banks tightened their due diligence requirements after the scandal, showing they responded and adapted — evidence they were not reckless but simply deceived.
The Argument Against You

The prosecution will argue: Banks have entire risk management departments whose job is to spot exactly this kind of overleveraging. With 80+ banks all lending simultaneously, no single bank had a full picture of NMC's total debt — a fragmented oversight that sophisticated lenders should have coordinated to prevent. Greed for interest income may have made them overlook warning signs.

You need to counter this. Think about: is a lender responsible for fraud committed by the borrower using legitimate financial instruments?

Guiding Questions
01
Should banks have independently verified NMC's finances rather than trusting EY's audit? Is that realistic?
Think about how many companies banks lend to and what verification would require.
02
The banks lost billions — does being a victim make you less responsible, or can you be both?
Consider whether negligence and victimhood can coexist.
03
What is the single strongest reason the banks bear less responsibility than the other three parties?
Build toward your closing argument — what's the core of your case?

Group 04 — Defend Your Person
The FRCFinancial Reporting Council — UK Audit Regulator
Least Responsible?
Who Are They
The Financial Reporting Council (FRC) is the UK body responsible for setting accounting and auditing standards, and for overseeing the quality of audits carried out by firms like EY. When EY audited NMC Health, those audits had to comply with standards the FRC sets and enforces. The FRC has the power to investigate audit failures and sanction firms. Despite NMC being listed since 2012, the FRC took no action until the fraud was already public — opening a formal investigation into EY's NMC audit only after the collapse in 2020.
Key Facts for Your Defense
  • The FRC oversees audit quality retrospectively — it investigates reported failures and complaints. It is not structured to monitor live audit engagements or flag problems in real time.
  • The FRC relies on audit firms to self-report concerns and cooperate with oversight. EY provided no warnings and raised no red flags with the regulator at any point during the NMC engagement.
  • NMC's actual financial operations, banking relationships, and transactions were almost entirely in the UAE — outside the reach of UK audit standards and FRC jurisdiction.
  • The FRC did respond once the fraud was exposed — opening a formal investigation into EY's conduct of the NMC audit, which contributed directly to the £105.5M settlement.
  • Setting accounting standards is fundamentally different from enforcing them at every individual company. The FRC cannot be expected to independently verify every listed firm's audit compliance.
  • The FRC has since strengthened its audit oversight regime and pushed for major audit reform as a direct result of high-profile failures including NMC — showing institutional accountability.
The Argument Against You

The prosecution will argue: The FRC's entire purpose is to ensure audit quality — and EY's clean sign-off on NMC's accounts was the single most important factor that enabled the fraud to continue. If audits are the system's safeguard, the body responsible for audit standards must share accountability when those audits catastrophically fail. Muddy Waters found the red flags in public documents. A regulator with the mandate and tools to review EY's audit quality had every reason to look closer, and didn't.

You need to counter this. Think about: is a standards-setter responsible when a regulated firm breaks those standards in secret?

Guiding Questions
01
Is the FRC responsible for the fraud if EY followed the standards the FRC set — but those standards weren't strong enough to catch it?
Think about whether the problem was the rules, or the auditor who broke them.
02
Should the FRC have proactively reviewed EY's audit quality on NMC before the collapse? Is that a realistic expectation?
Consider how many firms the FRC oversees and what proactive review would require.
03
What is the single strongest reason the FRC bears less responsibility than the other three parties?
Build toward your closing argument — what's the core of your case?
